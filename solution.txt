PROBLEM 1: TOWER OF HANOI
--------------------------

The idea:
  You have 3 pegs and n disks of different sizes, all starting on peg A
  stacked biggest-to-smallest. Goal is to get them all to peg C.
  Rules: move one disk at a time, can't put a big disk on a small one.

Why recursion works here:
  To move n disks from A to C, you can think of it as:
    - "get the top n-1 disks out of the way onto B"
    - "move the bottom disk to C"
    - "move that n-1 stack from B to C"
  
  And the "get n-1 disks somewhere" part is literally the same problem
  just smaller. So the function calls itself. That's the recursion.
  
  Base case: n=1, just move it. No sub-problem needed.

Worked example with 3 disks:

  Start:  A=[3,2,1]  B=[]  C=[]

  Move 1: disk 1  A -> C     A=[3,2]    B=[]     C=[1]
  Move 2: disk 2  A -> B     A=[3]      B=[2]    C=[1]
  Move 3: disk 1  C -> B     A=[3]      B=[2,1]  C=[]
  Move 4: disk 3  A -> C     A=[]       B=[2,1]  C=[3]
  Move 5: disk 1  B -> A     A=[1]      B=[2]    C=[3]
  Move 6: disk 2  B -> C     A=[1]      B=[]     C=[3,2]
  Move 7: disk 1  A -> C     A=[]       B=[]     C=[3,2,1]

  7 moves = 2^3 - 1. This is always optimal.

How the recursion tree looks (n=3):

  hanoi(3, A, B, C)
   |-- hanoi(2, A, C, B)
   |    |-- hanoi(1, A, B, C)  -> move disk 1  A->C
   |    |-- move disk 2  A->B
   |    |-- hanoi(1, C, A, B)  -> move disk 1  C->B
   |-- move disk 3  A->C
   |-- hanoi(2, B, A, C)
        |-- hanoi(1, B, C, A)  -> move disk 1  B->A
        |-- move disk 2  B->C
        |-- hanoi(1, A, B, C)  -> move disk 1  A->C

What's recursive vs iterative in the code:
  - hanoi() function = recursive (calls itself)
  - draw(), init loop = iterative (for-loops)


PROBLEM 2: CONWAY'S GAME OF LIFE
----------------------------------

What it is:
  A grid of cells. Each cell is alive or dead. Every "generation" you
  look at each cell, count how many of its 8 neighbors are alive, and
  decide whether it lives or dies.

The 4 rules (applied to all cells at the same time):
  1. alive + fewer than 2 neighbors  -> dies
  2. alive + 2 or 3 neighbors        -> stays alive
  3. alive + more than 3 neighbors   -> dies
  4. dead  + exactly 3 neighbors     -> becomes alive

Basically: 2 or 3 neighbors is the sweet spot for survival, 3 is what
it takes for birth. Everything else kills or stays dead.

Why iteration (not recursion):
  There's no self-similar sub-structure here. You're not breaking the
  problem into smaller versions of itself. You're just scanning a grid,
  counting things, and applying rules. That's loops.

Toroidal wrapping:
  The grid wraps around — if you go off the top edge you appear at the
  bottom, same for left/right. This avoids having to handle boundaries
  as a special case. The formula is:
    neighbor_row = (row + offset + total_rows) % total_rows

Step by step for a blinker (3x3, non-wrapping for clarity):

  Gen 0:        Gen 1:        Gen 2:
  . # .         . . .         . # .
  . # .         # # #         . # .     <- oscillates forever
  . # .         . . .         . # .

  The middle column has neighbors=1 on the top and bottom cells (they die)
  but the left and right of the middle row have exactly 3 neighbors
  each (they come alive). So it flips between vertical and horizontal.

Interesting patterns used in the code:
  - Glider: a 5-cell shape that "walks" diagonally across the grid
  - Gosper Glider Gun: 36 cells that produce a new glider every
    30 generations. Proves the Game of Life can grow without bound.

What's iterative in the code:
  Everything. The generation loop, the cell-scanning loop, the
  neighbor-counting loop, and the rendering loop are all for-loops.
  There's zero recursion.


RECURSION vs ITERATION — the short version
--------------------------------------------

Recursion: a function solves the problem by calling itself on a smaller
  version. State lives on the call stack. Risk of stack overflow for
  deep nesting. Natural when the problem has sub-problems that look
  the same as the original (like Hanoi, mergesort, tree traversal).

Iteration: explicit loops that scan/transform data. State lives in
  variables and arrays. No stack overflow worry. Natural when you're
  doing repeated passes over data (like cellular automata, sorting
  arrays, matrix operations).

Both are equivalent in power — anything you can write recursively can
be rewritten with a loop + explicit stack, and vice versa. The choice
is about which is clearer for the problem at hand.
